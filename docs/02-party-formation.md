# 02 — How to create a political party in Belgium

**Status:** orientation for founders. **Not legal advice.** Verify every filing with counsel specialised in electoral and non-profit law. Rules differ by election level (local / provincial / regional / federal / European) and by region.

## The big surprise

Belgium **does not have a classic “party registration law”** that creates parties as a formal legal status before they exist.

In practice:

1. A “party” is largely a **political association that presents candidates**.
2. **Ballot access** is the real gate (signatures or sitting MPs).
3. **Public funding** and spending rules kick in via the law on electoral expenses / party financing (notably the federal framework of 4 July 1989 and regional equivalents).
4. Almost all serious parties use one or more **VZW / ASBL** (non-profits) for money, staff, and accounting.

You can exist as a movement tomorrow. You become a **party in the Belgian sense** when you field lists and especially when you win seats (funding follows representation).

## Phase B — Legal vehicles

### 1. Movement (informal)

- Meet, publish, campaign ideas  
- **No** public party funding  
- Still subject to general law (privacy, defamation, assembly)

### 2. VZW / ASBL (almost mandatory for seriousness)

Use for:

- bank account  
- membership fees / donations (within legal limits)  
- staff contracts  
- receiving **public party funding** later (a designated non-profit is required for that channel)

Typical pattern of established parties: **multiple VZWs** (study centre, campaign, youth, …). Start with **one clean VZW**, add later.

**Do immediately after incorporation:**

- statutes with clear political purpose  
- board + conflict-of-interest rules  
- GDPR / membership register  
- accounting from day one (audit culture)

### 3. “Political party” for financing law

Federal financing law defines a party roughly as an association that **presents candidates** in the constitutional elections and aims to influence the popular will per its programme/articles. Exact obligations depend on level and whether you receive public funds.

**Practical rule:** once you plan lists, treat yourselves as under **party finance + campaign expense** regimes. Read the *sperperiode* (pre-electoral restricted period) rules for the election you target.

## Phase C — Getting on the ballot

### Federal Chamber (indicative — confirm per election circular)

From OSCE/ODIHR and Electoral Code practice:

- Lists are submitted to the main electoral office of the constituency.  
- Support by **200–500 voter signatures** depending on constituency population, **or** support by **3 members of parliament**.  
- A voter may support only **one list** (for that election type as regulated).  
- **Gender parity** on lists; top two candidates must not be same sex (quota rules — verify current text).  
- No classic UK-style deposit in the same form; the **signature hurdle** is the filter.  
- **5% threshold** applies in constituencies (verify current Electoral Code for your election).

### Flemish Parliament / regional

Separate calendars and signature rules — **do not assume federal numbers apply**. Pull the official circular the year of the election from Flemish authorities.

### Municipal / provincial

- Often the first realistic entry for new lists  
- Signature / support requirements are **local-code based**  
- Flanders municipal elections run on a **six-year** cycle (e.g. Oct 2024 → next expected **2030** — confirm).  
- Do **not** plan around outdated “2026 municipal” assumptions without checking.

### Gender & list form

Belgian lists have strict form requirements. A single paperwork error can kill a list. Use a **checklist + lawyer + dry run** six months early.

## Phase D — Money (the ugly truth)

| Source | Reality |
|--------|---------|
| Private donations | **Heavily restricted** compared to US; ceilings and transparency rules apply |
| Membership fees | Useful but small at start |
| Public funding | Effectively requires **parliamentary representation** (and designated VZW + accounts) |
| Campaign spending | Caps during regulated periods; ban/limit on certain ads (e.g. paid broadcast ads historically restricted) |

**Implication:** early years are **cheap authenticity + earned media + volunteer army**, not Super Bowl ads. The AI/media strategy is not optional — it is the only affordable awareness engine.

## Phase E — Recommended entry strategy

### Flanders-first (recommended)

Why:

- One language machine  
- Clear media market (VRT, HLN, De Tijd, podcasts, X)  
- Concentrated signature logistics  
- Federal lists can still be built constituency by constituency later  

### Brussels second

Harder (bilingual competition, different party system), high media value.

### Wallonia third

Different political culture and party brands; do not fake bilingual depth on day one. Better: **allied francophone economic-liberal list** later than a hollow “national” brand.

### Local beachheads

Pick 3–10 municipalities with:

- high share of net taxpayers / entrepreneurs  
- weak liberal offer  
- organisers who actually live there  

Win **local seats → proof of competence → regional credibility**.

## Common failure modes

1. **Brand without signatures** — viral, zero lists  
2. **Lists without programme** — protest vote that evaporates  
3. **Illegal money** — career-ending  
4. **Founder cult** — dies when founder burns out  
5. **All Belgium day one** — zero depth, bilingual mediocrity  
6. **Coalition addiction** — sell reform for a secretary of state title
